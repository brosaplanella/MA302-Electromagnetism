5  Applications of magnetostatics

TipAims of the chapter

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Understand the concept of surface currents and their role in magnetostatics.

  • Explain why magnetic monopoles cannot exist.

  • Understand the concept of magnetic dipoles.

  • Calculate the force between currents and between dipoles.

  • Explain how permanent magnets work.

Having introduced the basic concepts for magnetostatics, let’s now put them into practice to better understand magnetism.

5.1 Surface currents

In Section 3.2 we talked about surface charges and their consequences on the continuity of the electric field. We now want to do a similar reasoning but for surface currents instead. As the name suggests, surface currents are currents localised at a surface only. One way to think about it is to picture the surface as an infinite collection of wires, through which current flows. We denote the surface current density as {\boldsymbol{K}}, and it has dimensions of current per unit length.

Proposition 5.1 Consider the magnetic field generated by a surface current {\boldsymbol{K}} on a surface \Sigma. Then the magnetic field {\boldsymbol{B}} must be continuous across \Sigma in the normal direction, but not in the tangential direction.

In particular, the discontinuity can be written as

{\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ - {\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^- = \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{K}}. \tag{5.1}

Here the subscripts + and - represent different sides of the surface (arbitrarily labelled), and {\boldsymbol{n}} is the normal unit vector to the surface pointing into the + side.

Proof. The proof follows very similarly to the proof of Proposition 3.2, and we will also use the pillbox and loop arguments sketched in Figure 3.1.

Let’s start with the pillbox to show that the magnetic field is continuous across the surface. Integrating Gauss’ law for magnetic fields (Theorem 4.2) over the cylinder and applying the divergence theorem (Theorem 1.2) we obtain

0 = \int_\Omega \nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}\; {\mathrm{d}}V = \int_{\partial \Omega} {\boldsymbol{B}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}\; {\mathrm{d}}A.

Collapsing the cylinder onto the surface in the same way we did in the proof of Proposition 3.2 we conclude that

{\boldsymbol{B}}^+ \cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}- {\boldsymbol{B}}^- \cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}= 0.

Now let’s consider the closed loop C enclosing a surface S. Integrating Ampère’s law (Theorem 4.3) over S and applying Stokes’ theorem (Theorem 1.1) yields

\int_S \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{J}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}A = \int_S \left( \nabla \times {\boldsymbol{B}}\right) \cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S {\mathrm{d}}A = \int_C {\boldsymbol{B}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{t}}\; {\mathrm{d}}s,

where {\boldsymbol{n}}^S is the normal unit vector to the surface S enclosed by the loop. Collapsing the loop onto the surface, as we did for surface charges, we obtain

\int_{C^+} {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ \cdot {\boldsymbol{t}}^+ \; {\mathrm{d}}s - \int_{C^-} {\boldsymbol{B}}^- \cdot {\boldsymbol{t}}^- = \int_S \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{J}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}A,

where C^+ and C^- denote the upper and lower branches of the loop after collapsing it to the surface, with tangent vectors {\boldsymbol{t}}^+ and {\boldsymbol{t}}^-, respectively. Note, however, that C^+ \equiv C^- and {\boldsymbol{t}}^+ = - {\boldsymbol{t}}^-. Moreover, the current is all concentrated on the surface, so we have

\int_S \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{J}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}A = \int_{C^+} \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{K}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}s.

Combining everything we get

\int_{C^+} \left( {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ \cdot {\boldsymbol{t}}- {\boldsymbol{B}}^- \cdot {\boldsymbol{t}}\right) \; {\mathrm{d}}s = \int_{C^+} \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{K}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}s, \tag{5.2}

where we have dropped the + in {\boldsymbol{t}}^+ to simplify notation.

Recall, from Section 1.1.3, the orientation of surface. In this case, we require {\boldsymbol{t}}\times {\boldsymbol{n}}^S = {\boldsymbol{n}} (remember that {\boldsymbol{n}} is the normal unit vector to the surface where the current is, pointing in the + direction) or, equivalently, {\boldsymbol{t}}= {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \times {\boldsymbol{n}}. By the properties of the triple scalar product, we have

{\boldsymbol{B}}^+ \cdot \left( {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \times {\boldsymbol{n}}\right) = {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \cdot \left( {\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ \right),

so we can rewrite Equation 5.2 as

\int_{C^+} {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \cdot \left( {\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ - {\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^- \right) \; {\mathrm{d}}s = \int_{C^+} \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{K}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{n}}^S \; {\mathrm{d}}s,

and because this must hold for any loop we choose, we conclude that

{\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^+ - {\boldsymbol{n}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}^- = \mu_0 {\boldsymbol{K}}.

5.2 Magnetic monopoles

Let’s now consider more carefully the Gauss’ law for magnetic fields:

\nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}= 0.

What this equation tells us, physically, is that there is no such thing as a magnetic point charge (more commonly known as a monopole). To see this, let’s compare it with Gauss’ law for electric fields:

\nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{E}}= \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}.

In this case, the point charges (or electric monopoles, to continue the parallelism) are the sources and sinks of electric field. Therefore, going back to the magnetic field equation, we conclude magnetic monopoles can’t exist.

Mathematically it might still make sense to allow for magnetic monopoles, and from a physical point of view they also are a useful tool to understand some effects (especially going down to quantum mechanics). However, no one has ever been able to observe a magnetic monopole in real life, so one should not worry very much about them.

5.3 Magnetic dipoles

Now let’s talk about magnetic dipoles. Consider the magnetic vector potential generated by a current I flowing through a small circle C, as shown in #fig-magnetic-dipole. From Equation 4.6, we have

{\boldsymbol{A}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \int_C \frac{{\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}'}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}- {\boldsymbol{r}}'|}.

We now want to see what happens when we make this circle infinitely small. Wlog we take the C to be centred at the origin and lying on the (x,y)-plane. We can then parameterise it as {\boldsymbol{r}}' = (R \cos \theta', R \sin \theta', 0) for \theta' \in [0, 2\pi), where R > 0 is the radius of the circle. If we take a Taylor expansion in the limit of R \ll 1, we can write the integrand as

\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}- {\boldsymbol{r}}'|} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{(x - R \cos \theta')^2 + (y - R \sin \theta')^2 + z^2}} \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}} + R \frac{x \cos \theta' + y \sin \theta'}{\left(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \right)^\frac{3}{2}} + O(R^2). \end{aligned}

Using that {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}' = (-R \sin \theta', R \cos \theta', 0) {\mathrm{d}}\theta', we can write the vector potential as

\begin{aligned} {\boldsymbol{A}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) &= \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \int_C \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{(x - R \cos \theta')^2 + (y - R \sin \theta')^2 + z^2}} \right) {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}' \\ &= \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}} + R \frac{x \cos \theta' + y \sin \theta'}{\left(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \right)^\frac{3}{2}} + O(R^2) \right] (-R \sin \theta',R \cos \theta', 0) {\mathrm{d}}\theta'\\ &= \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi r^3} \left[\pi R^2 (-y, x, 0) + O(R^2) \right]\\ &= \frac{\mu_0 I}{4 \pi r^3} \left[\pi R^2 {\boldsymbol{e}}_z \times {\boldsymbol{r}}+ O(R^2) \right] \end{aligned} \tag{5.3}

The first term in the integral vanishes, as the integral is with respect to the tilde variables. Therefore, we are effectively taking the integral of a constant over a closed curve. In the last line we have just rewritten the result as a cross product for convenience.

Continuing with rewriting things in a convenient way, let’s introduce the magnetic dipole moment:

{\boldsymbol{m}}= I \pi R^2 {\boldsymbol{n}},

where {\boldsymbol{n}} is the vector normal to the surface enclosed by C (in our calculations above we have {\boldsymbol{n}}= {\boldsymbol{e}}_z due to our choice of coordinates). Now let’s take the limit R \to 0 while taking I \to \infty in such a way that I \pi R^2 remains constant. Then, as the higher order terms vanish, the potential Equation 5.3 can be written as

{\boldsymbol{A}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \frac{{\boldsymbol{m}}\times {\boldsymbol{r}}}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}|^3}. \tag{5.4}

Calculating the magnetic field of the dipole we obtain, after some algebra,

{\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \nabla \times {\boldsymbol{A}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \left(- \frac{{\boldsymbol{m}}}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}|^3} + \frac{3 ({\boldsymbol{m}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{r}}) {\boldsymbol{r}}}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}|^5}\right). \tag{5.5}

5.4 Magnetic forces

Now let’s turn our attention to magnetic forces. We will consider two cases: forces between currents and forces between dipoles.

5.4.1 Force between currents

We first consider the force between two current distributions {\boldsymbol{J}}_1({\boldsymbol{r}}) and {\boldsymbol{J}}_2 ({\boldsymbol{r}}) defined in \Omega_1 and \Omega_2, respectively. Take the magnetic field produced by {\boldsymbol{J}}_1, from the Biot-Savart law (Definition 4.4) we have

{\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} \int_{\Omega_1} \frac{{\boldsymbol{J}}_1({\boldsymbol{r}}') \times ({\boldsymbol{r}}- {\boldsymbol{r}}')}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}- {\boldsymbol{r}}'|^3} {\mathrm{d}}V'.

I we restrict ourselves to the case where we have a current I_1 through a curve (wire) C_1, we can write it as

{\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{4 \pi} \int_{C_1} \frac{{\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}_1 \times ({\boldsymbol{r}}- {\boldsymbol{r}}_1)}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}-{\boldsymbol{r}}_1|^3}.

Now let’s bring in the current distribution {\boldsymbol{J}}_2, which will experience a force due to {\boldsymbol{J}}_1 of

{\boldsymbol{F}}=\int_{\Omega_2} {\boldsymbol{J}}_2 ({\boldsymbol{r}}) \times {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}) {\mathrm{d}}V,

or, if we again restrict to the case where we have a current I_2 through a curve C_2,

{\boldsymbol{F}}= I_2 \int_{C_2} {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}).

Combining the two results we obtain

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} I_1 I_2 \int_{C_1} \int_{C_2} {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}_2 \times \left( {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}_1 \times \frac{{\boldsymbol{r}}_2 - {\boldsymbol{r}}_1}{| {\boldsymbol{r}}_2 - {\boldsymbol{r}}_1|^3} \right). \tag{5.6}

Notice the symmetry of the expression: if we were to compute the force on {\boldsymbol{J}}_1 produced by {\boldsymbol{J}}_2 we would reach the same result.

Example 5.1 (Two straight wires) Consider the case were we have to infinite, straight, parallel wires a distance d from each other, carrying currents I_1 and I_2 respectively. We want the force applied to the second wire.

Wlog we can write {\boldsymbol{r}}_1 = (0, 0, z_1) and {\boldsymbol{r}}_2 = (d, 0, z_2), and derive that {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}_1 = {\mathrm{d}}z_1 \; {\boldsymbol{e}}_z and {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}_2 = {\mathrm{d}}z_2 \; {\boldsymbol{e}}_z. Then, Equation 5.6 becomes

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} I_1 I_2 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} {\boldsymbol{e}}_z \times \left({\boldsymbol{e}}_z \times \frac{{\boldsymbol{r}}_2 - {\boldsymbol{r}}_1}{|{\boldsymbol{r}}_2 - {\boldsymbol{r}}_1|^3}\right) {\mathrm{d}}z_2 \; {\mathrm{d}}z_1.

We have {\boldsymbol{r}}_2 - {\boldsymbol{r}}_1 = d {\boldsymbol{e}}_x + (z_2 - z_1) {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{e}}_z, and using the properties of the cross product we obtain

\begin{aligned} {\boldsymbol{F}}&= \frac{\mu_0}{4 \pi} I_1 I_2 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{-d {\boldsymbol{e}}_x}{\left(d^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2\right)^\frac{3}{2}} {\mathrm{d}}z_2 {\mathrm{d}}z_1\\ &= - \frac{\mu_0}{2 \pi d} I_1 I_2 {\boldsymbol{e}}_x \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} {\mathrm{d}}z_2. \end{aligned}

The second integral is infinite, which makes sense as it represents the length of the second wire. But if we consider only the force per unit length on the wire, we get

{\boldsymbol{f}}= - \frac{\mu_0}{2 \pi d} I_1 I_2 {\boldsymbol{e}}_x.

If the currents have the same sign (i.e. I_1 I_2 > 0), then the force is attractive, while if the currents have opposite sign it is repulsive.

5.4.2 Force between dipoles

Let’s now calculate the force that a given magnetic field {\boldsymbol{B}} exerts on a dipole. We will use the same argument that we used in the definition of the dipole (Section 5.3), in which we took the current around an infinitely small circular wire. From the Lorentz force law (Definition 4.3), the force on the dipole is

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \int_C I {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}' \times {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}').

Using the parameterisation {\boldsymbol{r}}'=\left(R \cos \theta', R \sin \theta', 0 \right) for \theta' \in [0, 2\pi), and taking the Taylor expansion in the limit of small R we get

\begin{aligned} {\boldsymbol{F}}&= I \int_0^{2\pi} \left(-R \sin \theta', R \cos \theta', 0 \right) \times \left[ {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) + \frac{\partial}{\partial x} {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) R \cos \theta' + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) R \sin \theta' + O(R^2) \right] {\mathrm{d}}\theta' \\ &= I \pi R^2 \left[ {\boldsymbol{e}}_y \times \frac{\partial}{\partial x} {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) - {\boldsymbol{e}}_x \times \frac{\partial}{\partial y} {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) + O(R^3) \right] \\ &= I \pi R^2 \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} B_z({\boldsymbol{0}}) {\boldsymbol{e}}_x + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} B_z({\boldsymbol{0}}) {\boldsymbol{e}}_y - \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} B_x({\boldsymbol{0}}) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y} B_y({\boldsymbol{0}}) + O(R^3) \right)\right] \\ &= I \pi R^2 \left[\nabla B_z({\boldsymbol{0}}) - \left( \nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{0}}) \right) {\boldsymbol{e}}_z + O(R^3) \right]. \end{aligned}

Taking the limit R \to 0 while keeping the dipole moment {\boldsymbol{m}}= I \pi R^2 {\boldsymbol{e}}_z constant we obtain

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \nabla ({\boldsymbol{m}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}) - (\nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}) {\boldsymbol{m}}= \nabla({\boldsymbol{m}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}) = \nabla ({\boldsymbol{m}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}), \tag{5.7}

where in the final step we have used that \nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}= 0.

This force is conservative, and we can define the potential

V_\text{dipole} = - {\boldsymbol{m}}\cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}, \quad \implies \quad {\boldsymbol{F}}= - \nabla V_\text{dipole}.

The torque around the origin (using Equation 1.6) is

{\boldsymbol{\tau}}= \int_C {\boldsymbol{r}}' \times \left(I {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}' \times {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}') \right) = I \int_C {\mathrm{d}}{\boldsymbol{r}}' \left( {\boldsymbol{r}}' \cdot {\boldsymbol{B}}({\boldsymbol{r}}') \right).

By a similar argument as before, we can write

\begin{aligned} {\boldsymbol{\tau}}&= I \int_0^{2\pi} \left( -R \sin \theta' , R \cos \theta', 0 \right) \left[ B_x({\boldsymbol{0}}) R \cos \theta' + B_y({\boldsymbol{0}}) R \sin \theta' + O(R^2) \right] {\mathrm{d}}\theta'\\ &= I \pi R^2 \left[ (- B_y({\boldsymbol{0}}), B_x({\boldsymbol{0}}), 0) + O(R) \right], \end{aligned}

and taking the limit R \to 0 we obtain

{\boldsymbol{\tau}}= {\boldsymbol{m}}\times {\boldsymbol{B}}.

Note that both for the force and the torque, the expressions are analogous to those of the electric dipole, but replacing {\boldsymbol{p}} with {\boldsymbol{m}} and {\boldsymbol{E}} with {\boldsymbol{B}}. This also implies that magnetic dipoles will try to align with the magnetic field.

Example 5.2 (Force between dipoles) Now let’s consider the force between to dipoles with moment {\boldsymbol{m}}_1 and {\boldsymbol{m}}_2 separated by a distance {\boldsymbol{d}} (with |{\boldsymbol{d}}| = d). The magnetic field produced by the first dipole is given by Equation 5.5. Then, using Equation 5.7 we obtain that the force on the second dipole is

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \nabla \left( - \frac{{\boldsymbol{m}}_1 \cdot {\boldsymbol{m}}_2}{d^3} + \frac{3({\boldsymbol{m}}_1 \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}})({\boldsymbol{m}}_2 \cdot {\boldsymbol{d}})}{d^5} \right).

We can compute the explicit expression (note that, here, the gradient applies on the vector {\boldsymbol{d}}). After some careful1 manipulation, we obtain

{\boldsymbol{F}}= \frac{3 \mu_0}{4 \pi d^4} \left( ({\boldsymbol{m}}_1 \cdot \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}}) {\boldsymbol{m}}_2 + ({\boldsymbol{m}}_2 \cdot \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}}) {\boldsymbol{m}}_1 + ({\boldsymbol{m}}_1 \cdot {\boldsymbol{m}}_2) \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}} - 5 ({\boldsymbol{m}}_1 \cdot \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}}) ({\boldsymbol{m}}_2 \cdot \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}}) \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}} \right),

where \hat{{\boldsymbol{d}}} = {\boldsymbol{d}}/ d is the unit vector pointing from {\boldsymbol{m}}_1 to {\boldsymbol{m}}_2.

5.5 Application: permanent magnets

All the discussion we have had up to now was about magnetic fields arising from electric currents. But the magnets we are familiar with are the kind of thing we buy on our holidays to stick on our fridges. So where are the currents that produce the magnetic field in those magnets?

The answer to this question involves quantum mechanics. Even though quantum mechanics is out of scope for this model, let’s try to give some intuition about it.

The magnetism of such materials arises from the spin of the electrons. Electrons have an inherent angular momentum called spin. If you picture the electrons like tiny spheres you can imagine the spin in the same way as the Earth’s spin.2 It is this spin that produces a magnetic field. Typically, these magnetic fields point in all directions so their contributions basically cancel out at the macroscopic scale. However, when the spins of these many (many!) electrons align, the material produces a noticeable magnetic field.
